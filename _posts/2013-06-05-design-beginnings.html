Throughout my life, I've always liked to have a bunch of random (and not so random) projects going on in the background of my mainline life activities. So in high school, there was translating *The Hobbit *and doing layout for the French literary magazine. In college, there was planning and arranging for Friday Night Organ Pump, and another newspaper layout job. Oh, and learning Judaism. Lately I've realized that my project life has languished, so I've been on the lookout for new opportunities.

I'm really interested in tech cofoundering (hmm...) or first-tech-lead role in a startup in an area I value, such as education or SMB support. But, not having found that yet, I'm starting to look at freelance web. I'm loath to do standard-issue display sites, but I actually discovered that my own synagogue is looking to redo its hodge-podge site. They have a heavy emphasis (primary even) on continuing education, so there's a lot more potential than just a brochure-type site.

As such, I've started cracking into design. I bought Principles of Beautiful Web Design a while back, and now Design for Hackers. There's more exploration of certain historical topics in D4H than I would have asked for, but I really like Kadavy's presentation of design fundamentals of composition and proportion. These are slightly intuitive for me, but they're way out of my professional experience; I felt like he made them much more accessible in their depth, without diluting the concepts. His treatment of color was thorough also; this is a problem area for me, since my red-green colorblindness can leave me vulnerable to eyeburning combinations.

Anyway, the point of all this was, in approaching the synagogue site, I realized that they need a new logo, and in exploring that, found myself in an Apple Store experimenting with Paper for iPad. Again. It's really quite a masterpiece of UX, balancing functionality with approachability. In the five minutes I played with it, I was able to produce sketches of three concepts I've been working up in my head and physical sketchbook this week. As Travis explains in his review of the app, Paper's developers really achieved a remarkably poised balance in creating a UI for a really tricky product.

So I went and ordered me a refurb iPad Mini! It's been a decision a while in the making, but this clinched it. My first Apple product since I sold my iPhone 3GS a year or so ago. And now I get to play Impossible Road and Nightjar XD
